Section 1.3 Premises and Conclusions

In the previous section we said that arguments are built out of statements. Now we name the structural roles those statements play, and the relationship that makes a collection of statements an argument rather than just a list.

What a premise is, what a conclusion is.

Every argument has two kinds of statement in it. There is the statement being argued for — the claim the argument is for. There are also the statements offered as reason to accept that claim. The first is the conclusion. The others are the premises.

Definition 1.3.1.

A premise is a statement offered in support of another statement.
A conclusion is the statement being supported.
A small example.

Example 1.3.2. The library is closed.

P1: The library closes at nine.
P2: It is now ten past nine.
\(\therefore\) C: The library is closed.
The symbol \(\therefore\) is read “therefore.” We won’t use it constantly in prose, but you’ll see it whenever an argument is laid out cleanly, and it’s the symbol the proof checker we’ll use later uses to mark the conclusion line.
Two things to notice. First, an argument can have one premise or many. Most have several. Second, the conclusion is just another statement — there is nothing about its grammar or content that announces it as the conclusion. What makes it the conclusion is the role it plays in the argument: it’s the claim the others are aiming at. The same sentence could be a premise in one argument and a conclusion in another, depending on what the arguer is trying to do.

The support relation: intent and actuality.

When we say premises support a conclusion, we are saying two things at once, and it pays to keep them straight.
The first is about the intent of the arguer. When somebody offers an argument, they are saying: here are reasons; you should believe the conclusion because of them. The premise/conclusion structure is built out of intent. If I say “It is ten past nine, so the library is closed,” I am claiming that the first sentence is reason to believe the second. Whether anyone should be persuaded is a different question.
The second is about whether the support actually succeeds. The premises might genuinely give you reason to believe the conclusion (a good argument), or they might not (a bad argument). An argument can have its premises and its conclusion in their proper places, follow the rules of grammar, sound entirely persuasive — and still be a bad argument because the premises don’t actually support the conclusion. A bad argument is still an argument. It’s an argument whose support fails.
This is the question we promised in Section 1.1. Is this any good? Once we have premises and a conclusion in front of us, we can finally ask the question with an object in mind. Then, we can start to draw out some general properties from these specific arguments.

Indicator words.

In real texts, premises and conclusions are frequently flagged by short signal words. There’s a small set of them that show up over and over, and recognizing them is half the work of recognizing arguments.
Conclusion indicators — words and phrases that flag what comes next as a conclusion:
  • therefore
  • thus
  • hence
  • so (when used inferentially)
  • consequently
  • it follows that
  • which means that
  • because
Premise indicators — words and phrases that flag what comes next as a premise:
  • because
  • since
  • for (when used inferentially)
  • given that
  • as
  • the reason being
A short example with both kinds of indicator:
“Since the library closes at nine, and since it is now ten past nine, the library is closed.”
Two premise indicators (both since) flag the two premises. The unmarked sentence at the end is the conclusion.
A caveat worth taking seriously. Indicator words play other roles or dual roles. Because often introduces a premise, but it can also introduce a causal explanation — a different kind of speech that can sound similar and which we’ll work on distinguishing in the next section. Because, when it’s used as a premise indicator, also tells you what the conclusion is that’s being supported by that premise. For example, “You should support data center construction, because they are the most efficient way to fund public schools.” Because tells you both that “they are the most efficient way to fund public schools” is a premise and that “You should support data center construction” is the conclusion. So can introduce a conclusion (“It’s late, so we should go home”) or it can be filler (“So, anyway, the cat got out”) without doing inferential work. Don’t take an indicator as proof an argument is happening. Take it as a strong hint, then read carefully.
And one more case. Sometimes there are no indicator words at all. A passage can have a perfectly clear argument without a single therefore or because. You then have to identify premise and conclusion by content and intent: which sentence is the claim the writer is asking you to accept, and which sentences are the reasons offered? That’s a skill that takes practice. We’ll begin that practice in the next section.

Standard form.

Once we’ve picked premises and conclusion out of a passage, we lay them out cleanly. The convention we’ll call standard form in this book goes like this:
  • Each statement is on its own line.
  • Premises are labeled P1, P2, P3, … in the order they appear.
  • The conclusion is labeled C.
When an argument has more than one conclusion (which happens often in real texts — see the section below on sub-conclusions), each conclusion gets a number (C1, C2, …) and a parenthetical note saying which premises and earlier conclusions it follows from.
Two worked examples now, both about the same general topic. They’re worth pausing on, because together they make a point we’ll return to throughout the book.
Before we look at them, we need one piece of vocabulary that both examples use. Conditional sentences — sentences of the form “If [something], then [something else]” — have two named parts. The part following if is called the antecedent. The part following then is called the consequent. (In real text the then is often left implicit, but the structure is the same: “If it rains, the streets are wet” is doing the same work as “If it rains, then the streets are wet.”) We’ll work with conditionals in much more detail in Part III; for now the labels are enough to follow the examples below.

Example 1.3.3. Identifying antecedent and consequent.

In each of the following conditional sentences, the antecedent and consequent are labeled.
  • “If it is raining, then the streets are wet.” — Antecedent: “it is raining.” Consequent: “the streets are wet.”
  • “If there is no God, then life is meaningless.” — Antecedent: “there is no God.” Consequent: “life is meaningless.”
  • “If there is evil in the world, then God does not exist.” — Antecedent: “there is evil in the world.” Consequent: “God does not exist.”
The first is the argument we met at the end of Section 1.2:

Example 1.3.4. An argument for God’s existence.

P1: If there is no God, then life is meaningless.
P2: Life is not meaningless.
\(\therefore\) C: God exists.
This argument has the structure logicians call modus tollens: from a conditional and the denial of its consequent, we conclude the denial of its antecedent. We’ll meet this and other named argument forms in detail in Part III.
The second argument draws the opposite conclusion from the same general territory:

Example 1.3.5. An argument against God’s existence.

P1: If there is evil in the world, then God does not exist.
P2: There is evil in the world.
\(\therefore\) C: God does not exist.
This argument has the structure called modus ponens: from a conditional and the affirmation of its antecedent, we conclude the affirmation of its consequent. Like modus tollens, we’ll come back to it.
These two arguments make opposite claims. They take opposite sides of one of the deepest, longest-running disputes in human thought. Yet structurally, both are perfectly fine argument forms — meaning that if their premises were true, their conclusions would have to be true too. The disagreement between them isn’t a disagreement about logic. It’s a disagreement about whether the premises hold: whether life is meaningful, whether evil exists in a sense that’s incompatible with God. Settling those is hard work, much harder than checking the structure. But notice what the structural framing does for us: it makes it perfectly clear where the disagreement lies. The arguer who accepts the first argument and rejects the second is committed to defending “life is not meaningless” and challenging “if evil exists, God does not.” That’s a real position to hold; it’s also one a critic can engage with by attacking exactly those premises.
Structure first. Then we can ask what’s good about an argument and what isn’t, and have a productive disagreement when we disagree.

Many arguments have sub-conclusions.

Most real-world arguments are messier than P1, P2, \(\therefore\) C. Often a passage argues for an intermediate claim and then uses that claim as a premise for a further conclusion. When you see an argument laid out with C1, C2, and parenthetical notes like (from P1 and P2) or (from C1 and P3), that’s what’s going on. Conclusions can themselves serve as premises for later conclusions, and complex arguments are stacks of inferences. We’ll work with these explicitly in the next section and later. For now: don’t be surprised when a real argument has more than one conclusion in it.

Where we’re headed.

We now have the parts (statements), the roles those parts play (premises, conclusions), the relationship between them (support, intended and actual), and the standard layout (standard form, with sub-conclusions where the argument is layered). What we don’t yet have is much practice extracting all that from real text — text where the indicator words might be missing or misleading, where multiple things are happening at once, and where a passage that looks like an argument is sometimes really an explanation — a different kind of speech that uses the same shape. That’s the next section. The eavesdropping field exercise from Section 1.1 is about to start paying off.

If it doesn’t, write a one-sentence explanation of how you decided which statement was the conclusion.
