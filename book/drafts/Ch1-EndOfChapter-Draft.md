## Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced the basic apparatus for recognizing and analyzing arguments. The core ideas, section by section:

**What We Do With Language (§1.1).** Language serves many purposes — stating, asking, commanding, promising, expressing, performing, greeting, explaining, arguing — and most of them are not arguing. An argument is a special use of language in which reasons are offered in support of a claim. Arguing is not the same as persuading: persuasion aims to change someone's mind by any means; arguing offers reasons. An argument can persuade without being good, and a good argument can fail to persuade. This section also previews what makes an argument good (validity and soundness) and introduces the two main kinds of arguments: deductive (where the premises are intended to guarantee the conclusion) and inductive (where the premises are intended to make the conclusion probable).

**Statements (§1.2).** Arguments are built out of statements — assertoric sentences that are either true or false. Whether a sentence is a statement does not depend on whether anyone knows it is true, whether anyone can prove it, or whether people agree about it. The familiar fact-vs-opinion taxonomy is unreliable as a test for statementhood: many sentences people dismiss as "just opinions" are genuine statements (true or false), and many sentences people call "facts" are also someone's opinion (a belief they hold). The useful distinction is not fact vs. opinion but statement vs. non-statement.

**Premises and Conclusions (§1.3).** Every argument has premises and a conclusion. The conclusion is the claim being supported; the premises are the reasons offered in support of it. Indicator words — *therefore*, *so*, *since*, *because*, among others — often flag premises and conclusions, but they are not always present and not always reliable. Standard form lays an argument out with each statement on its own line (P1, P2, ... ∴ C). A conditional sentence ("If ..., then ...") has an antecedent (the "if" part) and a consequent (the "then" part). Two named argument forms were previewed: modus ponens (affirming the antecedent) and modus tollens (denying the consequent).

**Recognizing Arguments in the Wild (§1.4).** Arguments and explanations can look identical on the page but do different work. An argument moves the audience from accepted premises to a conclusion that is in question. An explanation provides reasons why something already accepted is the case. Two diagnostics: (1) Is the would-be conclusion something the audience already accepts (likely explanation) or something the speaker is trying to convince them of (likely argument)? (2) Is the speaker's posture "let me convince you" (argument) or "let me help you make sense of this" (explanation)? Real-world arguments are often layered: intermediate claims (sub-conclusions) serve as premises for further inferences. Standard form uses C1, C2, etc. with parenthetical notes to show which premises and earlier conclusions each sub-conclusion follows from.

## Key Terms

Listed alphabetically, with section references.

- **Antecedent** — In a conditional sentence ("If ..., then ..."), the part following "if." (§1.3)
- **Argument** — A set of statements in which one or more statements (the premises) are offered as reasons in support of another statement (the conclusion). (§1.1, §1.3)
- **Assertoric** — Describing a sentence that says something is the case; i.e., a sentence that is either true or false. Statements are assertoric sentences. (§1.2)
- **Conclusion** — The statement in an argument that the premises are offered to support. (§1.3)
- **Conclusion indicator** — A word or phrase (e.g., *therefore*, *thus*, *so*, *hence*, *consequently*, *it follows that*) that often signals that what follows is a conclusion. (§1.3)
- **Conditional sentence** — A sentence of the form "If ..., then ..." The "if" part is the antecedent; the "then" part is the consequent. (§1.3)
- **Consequent** — In a conditional sentence, the part following "then." (§1.3)
- **Deductive argument** — An argument in which the premises, if true, are intended to guarantee the truth of the conclusion. (§1.1)
- **Explanandum** — In an explanation, the thing being explained — the already-accepted fact whose cause or reason is being given. (§1.4)
- **Explanans** — In an explanation, the reason offered for why the explanandum is the case. (§1.4)
- **Explanation** — A use of language in which reasons are given for why something already accepted is the case. Distinct from an argument, which offers reasons for a claim that is in question. (§1.4)
- **Inductive argument** — An argument in which the premises, if true, are intended to make the conclusion probable but not certain. (§1.1)
- **Modus ponens** — An argument form: from a conditional and the affirmation of its antecedent, conclude the affirmation of its consequent. (§1.3)
- **Modus tollens** — An argument form: from a conditional and the denial of its consequent, conclude the denial of its antecedent. (§1.3)
- **Performative sentence** — A sentence that, when uttered in the right context, performs the action it describes (e.g., "I hereby resign"). Not a statement. (§1.1)
- **Premise** — A statement offered in support of a conclusion. (§1.3)
- **Premise indicator** — A word or phrase (e.g., *because*, *since*, *given that*, *for*, *the reason being*) that often signals that what follows is a premise. (§1.3)
- **Sound (argument)** — An argument that is valid and has all true premises. Previewed here; treated in detail in Chapter 2. (§1.1)
- **Standard form** — A layout for arguments in which each statement appears on its own line, premises are labeled P1, P2, etc., and the conclusion is labeled C. Sub-conclusions are labeled C1, C2, etc. with parenthetical notes showing what each follows from. (§1.3, §1.4)
- **Statement** — An assertoric sentence — a sentence that is either true or false, regardless of whether anyone knows which, can prove which, or agrees about which. (§1.2)
- **Strong (inductive argument)** — An inductive argument in which the premises, if true, make the conclusion highly probable. Previewed here; treated in detail in Part IV. (§1.1)
- **Sub-conclusion** — An intermediate conclusion in a layered argument that serves as a premise for a further inference. (§1.4)
- **Valid (argument)** — An argument in which, if all the premises were true, the conclusion would have to be true. Previewed here; treated in detail in Chapter 2. (§1.1)
