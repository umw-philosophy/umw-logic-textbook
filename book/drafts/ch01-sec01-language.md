## 1.1 What We Do With Language

Spend ten minutes listening in any public space and you will see people doing all sorts of things with language. They are telling stories, ordering food, complaining about traffic, complimenting each other's shoes, explaining how the parking app works, confessing crushes, lying about their weekends, asking for directions, and, even, on occasion, trying to convince someone of something using actual reasons.

That last activity, the giving-reasons one, is what we'll call *making an argument*, and it is the subject of this book. The other activities are largely where we find and make meaning with each other, but arguments are special in their own way as well. Arguments let us ask: *is this any good?* You can't ask that of "good morning" or "could you pass the salt?" You can ask it of "Since data centers demand immense power, we must expand the electrical grid," and the answer is not obvious until you stop and look closely (and might not be obvious even then).

This book is about how to look closely.

### A short list of things we do with language

Take all the things people do with language — every overheard sentence, every text, every email — and you can sort them, loosely, into a handful of types. The list isn't exact; many sentences do more than one thing at once, and philosophers of language have argued for a long time about exactly how the categories work. But for our purposes, a working list looks something like this.

**Stating.** "It's raining." "The library closes at nine." "Data centers consume a lot of power." Statements assert that something is the case. They can be true or false, and that is the property we are going to care about most.

**Asking.** "What time is it?" "Did you eat already?" Questions request information. They aren't true or false themselves, even though their answers might be.

**Commanding.** "Close the door." "Show your ID." Commands and requests try to get someone to do something. They're not true or false either, though there are good and bad ones.

**Promising.** "I'll be there by seven." "We'll consider your application by the fifteenth." A promise commits the speaker to some future action. It's evaluable in its own way — promises are kept or broken — but not as true or false at the moment of speaking.

**Expressing.** "Ouch." "What a beautiful day." Expressions report or perform an inner state. They're sincere or insincere, fitting or off-key, but again, not really true or false.

**Performing.** Some sentences make something happen just by being said in the right circumstances. "I now pronounce you married." "I name this ship the *Enterprise*." "I apologize." The philosopher J. L. Austin called these *performative* sentences and noticed they're a strange beast: they don't describe a marriage, a naming, or an apology — they constitute one.

**Greeting.** "Good morning." "Hey." "How's it going." A great deal of human conversation is just acknowledging that other humans exist and are not strangers. The semantic content of "How's it going" is roughly zero. The social work it does is enormous.

**Explaining.** "The reason your phone is hot is that you've been on it for two hours." "I'm late because there was an accident on Route 17." Explanations look a lot like arguments — they offer reasons connected to a claim — and we'll come back to that resemblance later.

**Arguing.** "You shouldn't take Route 17 — there was an accident, and traffic is backed up to the next exit." "We need to expand the grid because data centers are coming online faster than we can power them." An argument offers reasons in support of a claim. The reasons are called *premises*, the supported claim is called the *conclusion*, and the relationship between them is mostly what the rest of this book is about.

### Two senses of "argument"

The word *argument* is ambiguous in everyday English. It often refers to a verbal disagreement — two people exchanging insults, or yelling at each other about whether the runner was safe: "He was out!" "No way! He was safe!" That kind of dispute is not what we'll mean by *argument* in this book. We'll be using the word in the more specialized sense from the catalog above: an offering of reasons in support of a claim. From here on, when you see *argument*, that's the meaning to assume.

### Persuading and arguing

One thing you might notice in the list above is that I haven't said anything about *persuading*. That's deliberate. Persuasion isn't really a separate category — it's something a person can be trying to do across many of the categories at once. Importantly, you can persuade someone *without* arguing.

Advertising is the cleanest example. Most ads don't make arguments. They show you a beautiful person enjoying a beautiful product in beautiful light, and they hope that you, watching, will associate the product with the feeling of beauty. There's no premise, no conclusion, no reasoning offered. There's just an emotional pairing. If you walk away from the ad wanting the product, the ad has succeeded — but it did not argue.

Politicians do this too. So do friends, parents, salespeople, professors, and AI chatbots. A great deal of persuasion happens through repetition, charm, fear, social pressure, identity, and the sheer confidence with which a claim is delivered. None of this is arguing. None of it requires reasons.

The reverse is also true. You can make a careful, airtight argument and persuade exactly nobody. Anyone who has tried to talk an unwilling family member out of a strongly held belief knows the experience.

Logic is about the *reasoning* embedded in argumentative speech, not about whether the reasoning works on its audience. Whether arguments persuade is mostly a question of human psychology, and humans are persuaded by all sorts of things that have nothing to do with reasons. Whether arguments are *good* is a different question — a logical question — and it is the one we will spend the rest of this book learning to answer.

### What makes an argument good?

The central observation of the opening was that arguments are the kind of language use about which we can ask, "is this any good?" You can ask other questions of other kinds of speech: whether a promise was kept, whether a question was relevant, whether an explanation was satisfying, whether a greeting was warm. But "is this argument any good?" is a question that implies a specific structure, and the structure implied by that question is what the rest of the book is for.

What does it actually mean for an argument to be good?

A first answer: a good argument is one whose conclusion really does follow from its premises. If the premises support the conclusion, then accepting the premises gives you reason to accept the conclusion. If the premises do not support the conclusion, the argument is bad — even if every sentence in it happens to be true. Bad reasoning can land on a true conclusion by accident; good reasoning can sometimes land on a false conclusion if the premises were false. Both happen all the time. This is because a good argument isn't just about truth and falsity. 

Logicians have a specific vocabulary for these properties. The word *valid* means roughly "the conclusion really does follow from the premises," and it applies to the structure of an argument, not its content. And the word *sound* means "valid, and the premises are also actually true." For now, treat those as labels; we will fill in their precise meanings in the next chapter. The point is that those are the questions you can ask of an argument and not of "good morning."

### Two kinds of arguing

Arguments come in two broad kinds, and we'll discuss both in this book.

Some arguments are *deductive*. In a deductive argument, the premises are supposed to *guarantee* the conclusion — if the premises are true, the conclusion has to be true too, no exceptions. "All humans are mortal. Socrates is human. Therefore Socrates is mortal." If you accept the premises, you can't dodge the conclusion without contradicting yourself. Most of Parts I, II, and III of this book are about deductive reasoning.

Other arguments are *inductive*. In an inductive argument, the premises don't guarantee the conclusion, but they make it more *likely* than it would have been otherwise. "The last hundred swans I saw were white. So the next swan I see will probably be white too." The conclusion isn't forced; it's supported. Inductive arguments are the workhorses of science, statistics, medicine, and most everyday reasoning. Part IV of this book is about inductive reasoning, with statistical inference as its centerpiece.

Both kinds count as arguing. Both can be done well or badly. The standards of "good" are different in each case — what makes a deductive argument good is not the same as what makes an inductive one good — but in both cases we can ask the same question we asked above: *is this argument any good?*

### Where we're headed

To talk about arguments precisely, we need vocabulary for their parts. Arguments are built out of *statements* — claims that are either true or false — combined in particular ways. Some statements serve as premises, some as conclusions, and in compound arguments some serve as both. Not all sentences are statements, though: greetings, questions, and commands aren't, as the list above suggests. The next section gets specific about what statements are and how to recognize them, because we can't say much about arguments without first saying what they're made of.

> **Field Exercise 1.1.1 — Listen for ten minutes.**
>
> Find a public space — a coffee shop, a hallway between classes, a bus stop, a dorm common area — and listen for ten minutes without participating. (You can pretend to be on your phone. Nobody will notice.)
>
> While you listen, try to spot the different kinds of language use from the catalog above. You don't have to be exact, and many things you hear will fit more than one category at once. Notice especially:
>
> - How often you hear someone making an actual argument — that is, giving reasons in support of a claim.
> - Where the arguments tend to appear (between strangers, close friends, parents and kids, strangers with strong opinions about parking).
> - Whether anything you heard *sounded* like an argument but, on second thought, was doing a different job — an explanation, a complaint, a vent, a recital of grievances.
>
> You don't have to write any of this down right now. The point is just to start noticing the difference. We'll come back to it in an end-of-chapter writing prompt, where you'll be asked to report and reflect on what you heard.
