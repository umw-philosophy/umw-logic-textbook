# Argument Structure and Reconstruction

Chapter 1 taught us how to recognize arguments. That is already a real skill. Ordinary writing does not arrive with labels saying "premise here" and "conclusion here." It arrives as paragraphs, conversations, editorials, policy memos, posts, essays, speeches, and half-remembered things people say after dinner.

This chapter teaches the next skill: *argument reconstruction*. To reconstruct an argument is to take argumentative prose and lay out its structure clearly. We identify what the arguer is trying to get us to accept, which claims are supposed to support it, which claims are background or decoration, and how the pieces of support fit together. This work is slow at first, but it is the work that makes everything else in logic possible. We cannot ask whether an argument is valid, sound, strong, weak, fallacious, or persuasive until we know what argument we are actually evaluating.

## From Standard Form to Reconstruction

Standard form is the simple layout introduced in Chapter 1: one statement per line, premises labeled P1, P2, P3, and a conclusion labeled C. Reconstruction begins there, but it is more than copying sentences into a list.

### Reconstruction is a cleaned-up representation

A passage can contain an argument without being written like one. The conclusion may come first. A premise may be phrased as a rhetorical question. The author may repeat herself, add jokes, give background, or state an important assumption without spelling out exactly how it supports the conclusion. A reconstruction translates that mess into a clearer form.

> An *argument reconstruction* is a cleaned-up representation of an argument that identifies its premises, conclusion or conclusions, and support relations.

The word "cleaned-up" matters. A reconstruction is not a transcript. We are allowed to remove clutter, standardize wording, and make the support relations explicit. But cleaned-up does not mean rewritten into the argument we wish the author had given. The goal is to represent the author's reasoning fairly.

### The principle of charity

The guiding norm is often called the *principle of charity*. Interpret an argument in the strongest reasonable way. This does not mean pretending bad arguments are good. It means avoiding cheap victories that come from reading the arguer carelessly. If a sentence is ambiguous, choose the interpretation that best fits the surrounding passage. If the wording is sloppy but the intended claim is clear, repair the wording. If a modest unstated assumption is obviously doing work, make it explicit.

Charity has limits. You should not invent a new argument and hand it to the author. If the missing assumption is enormous, contested, or not something the author seems committed to, note the problem rather than quietly adding it. Reconstruction is fair-minded, not magical.

### Example: A first reconstruction

Consider this passage:

"If a rule prevents eligible citizens from voting without a strong public reason, then the rule should be repealed. This rule prevents eligible citizens from voting without a strong public reason. So this rule should be repealed."

A reasonable reconstruction:

P1: If a rule prevents eligible citizens from voting without a strong public reason, then the rule should be repealed.

P2: This rule prevents eligible citizens from voting without a strong public reason.

C: This rule should be repealed. (from P1 and P2, which are dependent)

Notice two things. First, the reconstruction does not add a separate line called "the argument." The argument is the premises and conclusion together. Second, the parenthetical note after the conclusion tells us how the support works. P1 and P2 are dependent. P1 alone only states a general principle about rules that prevent eligible citizens from voting. P2 alone only says that this particular rule has that feature. Each premise gives much weaker support (or none at all) without the other; together, they give the reason for the conclusion.

## What to Include, What to Leave Out

Real passages usually contain more than the argument. They contain set-up, examples, repetition, emotional emphasis, jokes, quotations, and background. Some of that material may be useful for understanding the passage, but not all of it belongs in the reconstruction.

> *Background information* is material that helps orient the reader but does not directly support the conclusion.

### The reconstruction filter

When deciding whether to include a sentence, ask: does this claim help support the conclusion, or does it merely help the reader understand the topic? If it supports the conclusion, include it. If it just sets the scene, leave it out of the reconstruction.

Leaving something out is not the same as saying it is unimportant. A sentence can be rhetorically important, historically interesting, or emotionally powerful without functioning as a premise. Logic is concerned with the support relation. We reconstruct that relation.

### Example: Background and argument

Passage:

"The old city hall was built in 1894, and its council chamber still has the original carved oak benches. The city should move public hearings to an accessible building until the elevator is repaired. Residents who use wheelchairs cannot reach the current chamber, and public hearings should be held where residents can actually participate."

Reconstruction:

P1: Residents who use wheelchairs cannot reach the current council chamber.

P2: Public hearings should be held where residents can actually participate.

C: The city should move public hearings to an accessible building until the elevator is repaired. (from P1 and P2, which are dependent)

The age of city hall and the carved oak benches do not belong in the reconstruction. They may make the passage more vivid, but they do not support the conclusion. The two access-centered claims do. Together they explain why the location of public hearings matters.

### Repetition and compression

Sometimes a passage states the same reason more than once. In a reconstruction, repeated material can often be compressed into a single premise. Suppose an author writes: "The system is too complicated. Ordinary people cannot understand it. The paperwork is confusing even for experts." In a reconstruction, that might become one premise: "The system is too complicated for ordinary people to navigate." That is not cheating. It is what a good reconstruction does: it gives us the argument without making us trip over every bit of prose.

## Dependent and Independent Support

Not all premises support a conclusion in the same way. Sometimes each premise gives its own reason to accept the conclusion. Sometimes two or more premises have to work together. This distinction matters both for reconstruction and for criticism: it tells us how important each premise is to the argument.

> Premises give *independent support* when each premise gives some reason to accept the conclusion on its own, without needing to be combined with another premise.

> A premise gives *dependent support* when the support it gives for a conclusion would be weaker without one or more other premises on which it depends.

### Why the distinction matters

The independent/dependent distinction helps us understand the significance of a premise. If a premise gives independent support, then removing it may weaken the argument without destroying the whole line of reasoning. If a premise is part of a dependent set, then removing it may break that line of reasoning entirely.

Think of two ways to support a shelf. Independent support is like two separate brackets: each one helps hold the shelf up. Dependent support is like a bracket and the screws that attach it to the wall: neither does the job by itself in the same way. In arguments, dependent premises are often a fact plus a principle, a conditional plus its antecedent, or a general claim plus a particular case.

### Example: Independent support

Passage:

"You should take the early bus. It is usually less crowded. Also, the early bus gets to campus before the coffee line gets ridiculous."

Reconstruction:

P1: The early bus is usually less crowded.

P2: The early bus gets to campus before the coffee line gets ridiculous.

C: You should take the early bus. (from P1 and P2 independently)

Either premise gives some reason to take the early bus. If P2 turns out false, P1 still gives support. If P1 turns out false, P2 still gives support. That is independent support.

### Example: Dependent support

Passage:

"If the only available exam time conflicts with a required lab, the department should offer a make-up exam. The only available exam time conflicts with a required lab. So the department should offer a make-up exam."

Reconstruction:

P1: If the only available exam time conflicts with a required lab, the department should offer a make-up exam.

P2: The only available exam time conflicts with a required lab.

C: The department should offer a make-up exam. (from P1 and P2, which are dependent)

P1 alone does not show that the department should offer a make-up exam, because it does not say the condition has actually been met. P2 alone does not show it either, because it does not state the policy principle. Together they do the work.

### The verbal diagram rule

Diagrams are helpful for many students, but the information in a diagram must also be stated in words. A reconstruction is not complete unless each conclusion says which earlier claims support it and whether that support is dependent or independent.

The parenthetical note is the accessible version of the diagram. It should be clear enough that a reader who never sees the arrows still knows the structure of the argument.

## Sub-Conclusions and Layered Arguments

Many arguments do not move directly from premises to one final conclusion. They build. A premise supports an intermediate claim; that intermediate claim then supports a further claim. Chapter 1 introduced these intermediate claims as sub-conclusions. Now we use them in reconstruction.

> A *sub-conclusion* is a claim that is supported by earlier premises and then functions as support for a later conclusion.

### Labels for layers

When an argument has layers, label the intermediate conclusions C1, C2, and so on. The final conclusion can be labeled C or the last numbered conclusion. What matters is consistency: the labels in the reconstruction and any diagram or explanation must match.

Every conclusion, including every sub-conclusion, should include a note saying what it follows from. That note is not busywork. It is the argument structure.

### Example: A layered argument

Passage:

"You should donate to the campus food pantry. It buys staple foods in bulk, so each dollar goes farther than it would at a grocery store. It also lets students choose what they need, which respects their dignity and reduces waste. Programs that use money efficiently and respect the people they serve deserve support."

Reconstruction:

P1: The campus food pantry buys staple foods in bulk.

C1: Each dollar donated to the campus food pantry goes farther than it would at a grocery store. (from P1)

P2: The campus food pantry lets students choose what they need.

C2: The campus food pantry respects students' dignity and reduces waste. (from P2)

P3: Programs that use money efficiently and respect the people they serve deserve support.

C: You should donate to the campus food pantry. (from C1, C2, and P3, which are dependent)

The final inference depends on all three pieces. C1 gives the efficiency claim. C2 gives the dignity-and-waste claim. P3 supplies the general principle connecting those features to the practical conclusion. If one of those pieces is missing, the final support is weaker or incomplete.

## Implicit Premises

Ordinary arguments often leave things unsaid. Sometimes that is harmless. We do not state every background assumption in every conversation, because conversation would become unbearable. But in reconstruction, an unstated assumption may need to be made explicit.

> An *implicit premise* is an unstated claim that must be supplied to make an argument's reasoning explicit.

### When to add one

Add an implicit premise when three conditions are met. First, the argument needs the claim in order for its support relation to make sense. Second, the claim is reasonably attributable to the arguer. Third, the wording can be kept minimal. The implicit premise should bridge the gap in the reasoning without smuggling in a totally new argument.

### Example: A modest implicit premise

Passage:

"The university should keep the shuttle running after evening classes. Some students have required labs that end after dark."

Reconstruction:

P1: Some students have required labs that end after dark.

P2: The university should provide safe transportation options for students whose required classes end after dark. (implicit)

C: The university should keep the shuttle running after evening classes. (from P1 and P2, which are dependent)

P2 is not stated, but it is a natural assumption behind the argument. Without it, P1 gives us a fact about students' schedules but not yet a reason for the shuttle policy. With it, the support relation is explicit.

### Do not over-reconstruct

The danger is overcharity. Suppose someone says, "We should ban all phones from campus because phones are distracting." It would be unfair to reconstruct the argument as if the author had also argued that phone bans improve mental health, increase friendship, reduce cheating, and restore civic life. Maybe those claims are relevant. Maybe they are even true. But they are not in the argument unless the passage gives us reason to attribute them to the arguer.

A good implicit premise makes the existing argument explicit. It does not replace the existing argument with a better one.

## Reconstructing Longer Passages

Longer passages are where reconstruction becomes genuinely useful. They also require patience. A long passage may contain background, examples, multiple arguments, explanations, objections, and side comments. The job is to find the argument that matters and represent it clearly.

### A working method

Use this procedure as a first pass:

1. Find the final conclusion. Ask: what is the author trying to get me to accept?

2. List the claims that seem to support that conclusion.

3. Remove background, examples, repetition, and rhetorical decoration unless they function as premises.

4. Look for sub-conclusions: claims that are supported by some earlier claims and then support the final conclusion.

5. Add modest implicit premises only when they are needed and reasonably attributable to the arguer.

6. Write the reconstruction with support notes after every conclusion.

### Example: A longer reconstruction

Passage:

"We should not institute a moratorium on data centers. New data centers are 3-10 times more efficient from a property tax standpoint than the next best developments. Property taxes pay for schools, which are already underfunded. New data centers are our best hope for fully funded schools. And they provide some decent construction jobs in the short term and tech industry jobs in the longer term. Environmental concerns are real, but overblown. Data centers consume only around 5% of US electricity. From what you hear, you'd think it was 50%."

Reconstruction:

P1: New data centers are 3-10 times more efficient from a property tax standpoint than the next best developments.

P2: Property taxes pay for schools.

P3: Schools are already underfunded.

C1: New data centers are our best hope for fully funded schools. (from P1, P2, and P3, which are dependent)

P4: New data centers provide decent construction jobs in the short term.

P5: New data centers provide tech industry jobs in the longer term.

C2: New data centers provide economic benefits. (implicit, from P4 and P5 independently)

P6: Data centers consume only around 5% of US electricity.

P7: Public discussion can make data centers sound as if they consume far more electricity than they do.

C3: The environmental concerns about data centers are real, but overblown. (from P6 and P7, which are dependent)

C: We should not institute a moratorium on data centers. (from C1 and C2 independently; C3 supports C by answering an objection)

This reconstruction does not preserve every sentence exactly as written. It organizes the reasoning. It also shows why each premise matters. P1, P2, and P3 work together to support C1: the tax efficiency of data centers matters because schools need property tax revenue. P4 and P5 each give some support for C2 (which is not explicitly stated) since short-term construction jobs and longer-term tech jobs are separate economic benefits. C3 is slightly different. It responds to an objection rather than giving the main positive reason for the conclusion. In a longer reconstruction, that matters: some claims build the case directly, while others defend it against an expected challenge.

This chapter developed argument reconstruction as a close-reading skill. The core ideas, section by section:

From Standard Form to Reconstruction (§2.1). Reconstruction is a cleaned-up representation of an argument. It identifies premises, conclusions, and support relations. It is not a transcript and not an excuse to invent a better argument. Charity requires us to represent the strongest reasonable version of the argument the author can fairly be understood as giving.

What to Include, What to Leave Out (§2.2). Not every sentence in a passage belongs in the reconstruction. Background, repetition, examples, and rhetorical decoration may help the passage work as prose without functioning as premises.

Dependent and Independent Support (§2.3). Independent premises each give support on their own. A premise is dependent when the support it gives would be weaker without another premise on which it depends. The distinction helps us understand the significance of each premise: removing a dependent premise may break a line of support, while removing an independent premise may merely weaken it.

Sub-Conclusions and Layered Arguments (§2.4). A sub-conclusion is supported by earlier premises and then serves as support for a later conclusion. Every conclusion and sub-conclusion should say what it follows from.

Implicit Premises (§2.5). Ordinary arguments often leave assumptions unstated. Add an implicit premise only when the argument needs it, the arguer is reasonably committed to it, and the wording can be kept minimal.

Reconstructing Longer Passages (§2.6). Longer passages require a method: find the final conclusion, list support claims, remove clutter, identify sub-conclusions, add modest implicit premises when needed, and write support notes after every conclusion.

## Key Terms

Listed alphabetically, with section references.

- **Argument reconstruction:** A cleaned-up representation of an argument that identifies its premises, conclusion or conclusions, and support relations. (§2.1)

- **Background information:** Material that helps orient the reader but does not directly support the conclusion. (§2.2)

- **Dependent support:** A support relation in which the support a premise gives for a conclusion would be weaker without one or more other premises on which it depends. (§2.3)

- **Implicit premise:** An unstated claim that must be supplied to make an argument's reasoning explicit. (§2.5)

- **Independent support:** A support relation in which each premise gives some reason to accept the conclusion on its own, without needing to be combined with another premise. (§2.3)

- **Principle of charity:** The norm of interpreting an argument in the strongest reasonable way consistent with what the arguer actually says and seems committed to. (§2.1)

- **Sub-conclusion:** A claim that is supported by earlier premises and then functions as support for a later conclusion. (§2.4)

- **Support note:** A parenthetical note after a conclusion that states which earlier claims support it and whether the support is dependent or independent. (§2.3)

## End-of-Chapter Exercises

### Exercise Set 1: Basic Reconstruction

For each passage, reconstruct the argument in standard form. Include a support note after the conclusion.

The city should add more crosswalks near campus. Students cross those streets constantly, and drivers often fail to slow down at the current crossings.

The cafeteria should keep one station open later. Evening labs end after the main dinner period, and students in those labs still need a real meal.

Do not rely on that weather app today. It has been wrong three days in a row, and the local forecast gives a different prediction.

### Exercise Set 2: Include or Omit

For each passage, identify the final conclusion, the premises that belong in the reconstruction, and any material that should be omitted as background or rhetorical decoration.

The train station opened in 1910 and still has its original tile floor. The city should repair it rather than replace it. The building is historically significant, and repairing it would cost less than constructing a new station.

Everyone loves a dramatic classroom demonstration. Still, the chemistry department should replace the old fume hoods. The current hoods fail inspection too often, and failed hoods expose students to unnecessary risk.

### Exercise Set 3: Dependent and Independent Support

Reconstruct each argument and indicate whether the support is dependent, independent, or mixed.

If the archive closes before noon, researchers who travel from out of town cannot finish their work. The archive closes before noon on Fridays. So out-of-town researchers cannot finish their work on Fridays.

The new advising system should be changed. It crashes during registration week. It also hides degree requirements behind too many menus.

The university should keep the writing center funded. Students who use it revise more effectively, and programs that improve student writing deserve stable funding. The center also gives peer tutors valuable teaching experience.

### Exercise Set 4: Sub-Conclusions

Reconstruct each passage using sub-conclusions where appropriate. Every conclusion must say what it follows from.

The discount brings in students who would otherwise skip the museum, and those students often return later with family members. That means the student discount builds future audiences. Programs that build future audiences are worth preserving, so the museum has a good reason to continue the discount.

Street trees reduce summer temperatures on sidewalks. Cooler sidewalks make downtown more usable during the hottest months. The trees also absorb stormwater, which helps reduce flooding after heavy rain. Downtown will be more livable if the city plants more street trees.

### Exercise Set 5: Implicit Premises

Reconstruct each argument. If an implicit premise is needed, include it and label it as implicit.

A few hundred dollars can be the difference between a student staying enrolled and dropping out. The emergency grant program gives students that kind of short-term help. The program protects student retention.

The student body has changed significantly since the survey was conducted. Old survey results are a poor guide to what current students need.

### Exercise Set 6: Longer Passage Reconstruction

Reconstruct the argument in each passage. Some statements may not be necessary. There may be implicit premises or sub-conclusions. If you use a diagram, the prose reconstruction must still state all support relations.

The new bus app is convenient for many riders, and it may reduce lines at the station. But some riders do not own smartphones, and others have limited data plans. A public transit system that makes access depend on owning a particular device or paying for a particular service excludes some of the people it is supposed to serve. The city can still encourage the app while keeping cash and card payment available on buses. So the app can be useful without becoming the only way to buy a ticket.

Automated tutors can answer routine questions quickly, and that is useful. But students often come to office hours because they are confused about what they are confused about. In those cases, a human instructor can listen, ask follow-up questions, and notice patterns the student cannot yet name. The hardest learning problems often require that kind of diagnosis. Automated tutors are useful supplements, but they are not replacements for office hours.
