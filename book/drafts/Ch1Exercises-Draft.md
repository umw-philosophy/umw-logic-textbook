## Exercise Set 5: Fact, Opinion, and Statementhood

The following classifications use the fact-vs-opinion taxonomy. For each one, explain what is wrong or incomplete about the classification from the point of view of Chapter 1.

1. "Factory farming is wrong" is not a statement because it is an opinion.
2. "Seven plus five equals twelve" is a fact, so it cannot be anyone's opinion.
3. "Universal basic income would make society fairer" is not true or false because people disagree about it.
4. "There are an even number of stars in the Milky Way" is not a statement because no one knows whether it is true.
5. "The campus bookstore opens at ten" is a statement, but "wealth taxes are just" is not, because only the first one can be proven.
6. "No claim can be both false and useful" cannot be used in an argument because it is about claims rather than about the world.
7. "The claim that 'we should raise the minimum wage' is just an opinion, so it cannot be a premise in an argument."
8. "'The Earth orbits the Sun' is a fact, not a statement."
9. "'Pineapple belongs on pizza' is neither true nor false because it is a matter of taste."
10. "You can't argue about morality because moral claims are just opinions."

## Exercise Set 6: Premises, Conclusions, and Indicators

For each passage below:

- Identify the conclusion.
- Identify the premise or premises.
- Note any indicator words.
- Lay the argument out in standard form.

1. Since the printer is out of toner and the handout is due in ten minutes, we need to send the file electronically.

2. The university should add more evening classes. Many students work during the day, and evening sections would make it easier for them to graduate on time.

3. We should not rely on the app's arrival time estimate, because it has been wrong three times this week.

4. If the grant deadline is Friday, then the application must be submitted this week. The grant deadline is Friday. Therefore, the application must be submitted this week.

5. Either the department hires another advisor or students will keep waiting weeks for appointments. Students should not have to wait weeks for appointments. So the department should hire another advisor.

6. The city should repair the sidewalks near campus. The cracked pavement is a hazard for wheelchair users, and several students have already reported injuries.

7. The museum should extend its hours on weekends. Families with school-age children can only visit on Saturdays and Sundays, and the museum currently closes at three, which is too early for most families.

8. Because the forecast calls for freezing rain, and because freezing rain makes the roads dangerous, you should leave early for your drive.

9. If tuition increases, then more students will take on debt. Tuition is increasing. Therefore, more students will take on debt.

10. The dining hall should offer more vegetarian options. A recent survey shows that 28% of students want vegetarian meals, but only 10% of the menu is vegetarian.

11. We cannot trust the poll results, since the sample was not random and fewer than 200 people responded.

12. Either the committee approves the budget by Friday or the project will be delayed. The committee will not approve the budget by Friday. So the project will be delayed.

## Exercise Set 9: Modus Ponens and Modus Tollens

For each argument below:

- Put the argument in standard form if it is not already.
- Identify whether it has the form of modus ponens, modus tollens, or neither.
- Name the conditional premise's antecedent and consequent.

1. If the alarm is armed, then opening the door will trigger it. The alarm is armed. Therefore, opening the door will trigger it.

2. If the greenhouse were heated, the tropical plants would still be alive. The tropical plants are not alive. Therefore, the greenhouse was not heated.

3. If the sculpture were made of bronze, then it would not be attracted to a magnet. The sculpture is attracted to a magnet. Therefore, the sculpture is not made of bronze.

4. If the river rises above the walkway, then the race will be rerouted. The river is rising above the walkway. So the race will be rerouted.

5. If the course required a textbook, it would appear on the bookstore list. The course does not require a textbook. Therefore, it does not appear on the bookstore list.

6. If the proposal reduces advising delays, then we should consider adopting it. We should consider adopting it. Therefore, the proposal reduces advising delays.

7. If class is canceled, then the assignment deadline will be extended. Class is canceled. Therefore, the assignment deadline will be extended.

8. If the building meets the fire code, then the sprinkler system is functional. The sprinkler system is not functional. Therefore, the building does not meet the fire code.

9. If the study abroad program requires a 3.0 GPA, then students below 3.0 are ineligible. The program requires a 3.0 GPA. Therefore, students below 3.0 are ineligible.

10. If the restaurant passed its health inspection, then the kitchen is clean. The restaurant passed its health inspection. Therefore, the restaurant is a good place to eat.

## Exercise Set 11: Extracting Arguments From Realistic Prose

For each passage below, use the procedure from Section 1.4:

1. Read for the gist.
2. Find the conclusion.
3. Find the premises.
4. Lay the argument out in standard form.
5. Check whether anything should be left out because it is background, orientation, or a separate language use.

Do not evaluate the argument yet.

1. Most students think office hours are only for emergencies, but that is a mistake. Students should visit office hours early in the semester. Early visits make it easier to ask for help later, and they give professors a better sense of what students are finding confusing.

2. The city should add protected bike lanes on the road next to campus. Cars regularly pass cyclists with less than three feet of space, and the current painted lane disappears at the busiest intersection. A protected lane would make commuting safer for students and staff.

3. We should replace the old projector in Monroe Hall. It cuts out during class, the replacement bulbs are no longer easy to order, and several instructors have stopped using media clips because they cannot trust the equipment.

4. The policy should be revised before next semester. It was written before online advising appointments became common, and it does not say who is responsible when a student misses a remote meeting because of a technology failure.

5. The committee should not postpone the vote again. Everyone has had the proposal for three weeks. The remaining objections are about implementation details, not about the main principle, and those details can be handled after approval.

6. The campus shuttle schedule should be revised. The current routes were designed five years ago, before the new science building opened on the south end of campus. Students now have to walk twenty minutes from the nearest shuttle stop to reach their labs, and several have reported being late to class as a result.

7. College athletes should be allowed to profit from their name, image, and likeness. They generate millions in revenue for their schools, and other students are free to earn money from their talents.

8. The university should not eliminate the foreign language requirement. Research consistently shows that studying a second language improves cognitive flexibility. Many employers prefer candidates who speak more than one language. And exposure to another language and culture is part of what a liberal arts education is for.

9. Standardized test scores should be optional in admissions. Scores correlate more strongly with family income than with college performance, and many high-performing students test poorly due to anxiety.

10. The department should keep offering the senior seminar in person rather than moving it online. The seminar depends on spontaneous discussion, and online formats make it harder for students to build on each other's comments in real time.

## Exercise Set 12: Layered Arguments and Sub-Conclusions

Each passage below contains a layered argument. Lay it out in standard form using P1, P2, C1, C2, and so on. Include parenthetical notes saying what each conclusion follows from.

1. The university should expand evening tutoring. Many students who need tutoring work during the day. Students who work during the day cannot attend most current tutoring sessions. So the current schedule excludes many of the students who need help. Expanding evening tutoring would make academic support available to more students.

2. We should move the club meeting to Thursday. The room is already reserved for another group on Wednesday, so we cannot meet in our usual room then. If we meet somewhere else on Wednesday, several members will not know where to go. Thursday is the only night this week when the usual room is available.

3. The city should install better lighting near the crosswalk. Drivers often fail to see pedestrians there at night. Poor visibility makes collisions more likely. Better lighting would improve visibility, and improving visibility would make the crosswalk safer.

4. The department should keep the methods course as a requirement. Students who take it write stronger senior theses, because the course teaches research design before students begin independent projects. Stronger senior theses make the major more valuable. A required methods course also gives transfer students a shared foundation with students who began the major earlier.

5. We should not use attendance software that tracks students' locations. The software collects sensitive data. Sensitive data can be misused or exposed in a breach. Tools that create serious privacy risks should be avoided unless they are necessary. This software is not necessary, since professors can take attendance without location tracking.

6. The city should build a new park near the university. The nearest park is two miles away, and students without cars cannot easily reach it. A nearby park would improve mental health, because access to green space reduces stress. Reduced stress leads to better academic performance.

7. We should require all first-year students to take a critical thinking course. Students arrive with widely varying reasoning skills. A shared course would give everyone a common vocabulary for evaluating arguments. A common vocabulary makes class discussions in other courses more productive. More productive discussions improve learning across the curriculum.

8. The software should be replaced. It crashes at least once per week during peak hours. Each crash disrupts service for roughly 500 users. Frequent disruptions erode trust in the system. A system that users do not trust will not be used, and a system that is not used wastes the money spent on it.

## Exercise Set 13: Mixed Review

For each passage below, complete all tasks that apply.

- Classify the passage as an argument, explanation, or another language use.
- Identify all statements in the passage.
- If it is an argument, put it in standard form.
- If it is an explanation, identify the explanandum and explanans.
- If it includes a conditional, identify its antecedent and consequent.
- If it includes a sub-conclusion, label it.

1. If the storm reaches campus before noon, then afternoon labs should be canceled. The storm is projected to arrive by 11:30, so afternoon labs should be canceled.

2. The lab smelled strange because someone left a burner on after class.

3. Please do not leave burners on after class.

4. We should not schedule the exam on the Monday after break. Many students travel that weekend, and travel delays would make the exam harder for reasons unrelated to course learning.

5. If the collaboration policy applies to group chat notes, then students must list those notes in their acknowledgments. The collaboration policy does apply to group chat notes. Therefore, students must list those notes in their acknowledgments.

6. Students must list group chat notes in their acknowledgments because the collaboration policy applies to them.

7. The classroom was moved because the projector broke. The new classroom is in Combs.

8. Since the current policy does not mention online quizzes, students and instructors are interpreting it differently. If a policy is being interpreted differently by the people governed by it, then it should be clarified. Therefore, the current policy should be clarified.

9. If the committee meets on Friday, then the proposal will be voted on before the deadline. The proposal will not be voted on before the deadline. Therefore, the committee is not meeting on Friday.

10. The concert was canceled because the headlining band's bus broke down on the interstate.

11. You should apply for the internship. The application deadline is next week, the position pays well, and it is directly related to your major.

12. Since the fire alarm sometimes malfunctions, we cannot always tell from the alarm alone whether there is an actual fire. But since we cannot always tell, and since the cost of ignoring a real fire is catastrophic, we should treat every alarm as real until confirmed otherwise.

## Exercise Set 14: Short Writing Prompts

Choose two of the following prompts. Each response should be one to two paragraphs.

1. Describe a case where someone persuaded you without giving an argument. What made the persuasion work?

2. Describe a case where someone gave you an argument that did not persuade you. What were the premises and conclusion?

3. Find a short passage from a news article, opinion piece, advertisement, syllabus, campus email, or social media post. Classify it as an argument, explanation, or something else. Defend your classification.

4. Return to the listening exercise from Section 1.1. Report one example of language use you noticed and classify it using the categories from the chapter.

5. Explain why "statement" is a more useful category for logic than "fact" or "opinion."

6. Write your own short layered argument with at least two premises, one sub-conclusion, and one final conclusion. Then lay it out in standard form.

7. Pick one of the arguments you put in standard form in Exercise Sets 6 or 7. Without yet using the formal tools from later chapters, write a paragraph assessing whether you think the premises actually support the conclusion. What would someone who disagrees with the conclusion say about the premises?

8. Find a passage from a textbook in another course you are taking. Determine whether the passage is making an argument, giving an explanation, or doing something else. Quote the passage, classify it, and explain your reasoning.

## Optional Instructor Notes

These exercises are designed to practice only the skills explicitly introduced in Chapter 1:

- classifying uses of language;
- distinguishing argument from persuasion;
- identifying statements and non-statements;
- separating truth from knowledge and consensus;
- avoiding the fact-vs-opinion taxonomy as a test for statementhood;
- identifying premises, conclusions, support relations, and indicator words;
- putting arguments in standard form;
- identifying antecedents and consequents in conditionals;
- recognizing simple modus ponens and modus tollens forms;
- distinguishing arguments from explanations;
- identifying explanandum and explanans;
- extracting arguments from prose;
- representing layered arguments with sub-conclusions.
