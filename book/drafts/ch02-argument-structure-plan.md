## Pedagogical Role

Chapter 1 gets students to recognize arguments, statements, premises, conclusions, indicators, standard form, and the argument/explanation contrast. Chapter 2 should turn that recognition skill into a reconstruction skill.

The chapter should teach students how to take an ordinary passage and produce a clean representation of its argumentative structure. This is the bridge between "there is an argument here" and later questions like "is this argument valid?", "is this argument sound?", "which premises are doing the work?", and "where does the reasoning fail?"

This chapter is also the natural home for the first full treatment of argument diagrams, dependent support, independent support, sub-conclusions, implicit premises, and unnecessary/background material.

## Skills From Existing Assignments

Short Writing 1 asks students to:

- Find an argument in a short passage.
- Label premises and conclusions.
- Produce their own argument or evaluate an AI-generated argument.
- Avoid treating "the argument" as a separate item beyond the premises and conclusion.

Short Writing 2 asks students to:

- Reconstruct an argument from a specified paragraph.
- Reconstruct one additional argument from a longer essay.
- Label premises and conclusions.
- Diagram the relation between premises and conclusions.
- Justify each conclusion by saying which premises or sub-conclusions support it.

Short Writing 3 asks students to:

- Work with fallacies.
- Reconstruct and diagram a short philosophical argument.
- At minimum, identify which premises are dependent.

Exam 1 Part IV asks students to:

- Reconstruct and diagram all arguments in a longer passage.
- Omit statements that are not necessary to the argument.
- Identify implicit premises or conclusions when needed.
- Recognize that a passage may contain more than one argument.
- Indicate which premises are dependent.
- Indicate which premises support which conclusions, either through a diagram or prose explanation.

Study Guide Part IV adds:

- If there is more than one argument, try to construct the argument that supports the final conclusion of the passage.
- All conclusions must be justified.
- Diagrams should distinguish independent support from linked/dependent support.
- Labels in the reconstruction and diagram must match.

## Proposed Chapter Arc

### 2.1 From Standard Form to Reconstruction

Purpose: connect Chapter 1 standard form to the more difficult task of reconstructing ordinary prose.

Core ideas:

- Standard form is a cleaned-up representation, not a transcript.
- Reconstruction requires judgment.
- The goal is charity and clarity: preserve the reasoning while removing clutter.
- Premises and conclusions are the whole argument; do not add a separate line called "the argument."

Likely examples:

- A short AI creativity argument modeled on Short Writing 1, but with a new passage.
- A student-generated argument example.

### 2.2 What to Include, What to Leave Out

Purpose: teach students to separate argumentative content from background, illustration, rhetoric, repetition, and explanatory detail.

Core ideas:

- Some sentences are scene-setting or background.
- Some sentences repeat a claim already made.
- Some sentences intensify or decorate the argument without adding support.
- Reconstruct only what contributes to the support relation.

Likely examples:

- A short public-policy paragraph with background and one clear argument.
- A longer passage with several claims, only some of which support the final conclusion.

### 2.3 Dependent and Independent Premises

Purpose: teach the first real argument-diagram distinction.

Core ideas:

- Independent premises each give their own support to a conclusion.
- Dependent premises work together; separated, each may fail to support the conclusion.
- Some conclusions depend on a combination of a factual claim and a value/principle claim.
- The text reconstruction must state support relations explicitly; diagrams are visual supplements, not the primary record of the argument's structure.

Likely examples:

- A simple independent-support argument: two separate reasons to take a course.
- A simple dependent-support argument: a conditional plus antecedent; a general principle plus case fact.
- Contrast pairs using the same conclusion but different support structure.

### 2.4 Sub-Conclusions and Layered Arguments

Purpose: make explicit the structure used in the Oxfam example from the study guide.

Core ideas:

- A sub-conclusion is supported by earlier premises and then supports a later conclusion.
- A passage may contain several layers of support.
- Label sub-conclusions as `C1`, `C2`, etc. when useful.
- Every conclusion and sub-conclusion should say what it follows from.
- If premises are dependent, the reconstruction should say so in words, not only by placing them together in a diagram.

Likely examples:

- A fresh charity/donation example parallel to the Oxfam structure.
- A short climate/AI or education-policy example with one sub-conclusion feeding a final conclusion.

### 2.5 Implicit Premises and Charitable Reconstruction

Purpose: teach students how to handle missing assumptions without inventing a new argument.

Core ideas:

- Ordinary arguments often leave premises unstated.
- Add an implicit premise only when the argument needs it and the speaker is reasonably committed to it.
- Use neutral, minimal wording.
- Do not use reconstruction as an excuse to make the argument stronger than the speaker's actual reasoning permits.

Likely examples:

- A short argument where a value premise is implicit.
- A short argument where a bridge principle is implicit.
- A bad over-reconstruction that adds too much.

### 2.6 Reconstructing Longer Passages

Purpose: prepare students for Exam 1 Part IV.

Core ideas:

- Longer passages may contain multiple arguments.
- The task is usually to find the argument supporting the final conclusion.
- Work in stages: find final conclusion, list candidate support claims, remove background, identify sub-conclusions, diagram support.
- If there are multiple arguments, either separate them or show how one supports the final conclusion.

Likely examples:

- A medium-length passage in the style of the Border Patrol exam passage, but newly written.
- One worked reconstruction plus one practice passage.

## Candidate Chapter Definitions

- **Argument reconstruction**: A cleaned-up representation of an argument that identifies its premises, conclusion(s), and support relations.
- **Independent support**: A support relation in which each premise gives some reason for the conclusion on its own.
- **Dependent support** or **linked support**: A support relation in which two or more premises work together to support a conclusion.
- **Sub-conclusion**: A claim that is supported by earlier premises and then functions as support for a later conclusion.
- **Implicit premise**: An unstated claim that must be supplied to make the argument's reasoning explicit.
- **Background information**: Material in a passage that may help orient the reader but does not directly support the conclusion.

## Diagramming Convention To Settle

Use statement labels consistently between reconstruction and diagram.

Accessibility constraint: diagrams are useful visual summaries, but they cannot be the only place where support relations are represented. The canonical reconstruction should be fully understandable without seeing the diagram. Every conclusion should include a parenthetical support note, and dependent premises should be verbally identified.

Possible text-first convention:

```text
P1 + P2 -> C1
C1 + P3 -> C2
P4 -> C2
```

Equivalent prose version:

```text
P1: ...
P2: ...
C1: ... (from P1 and P2, which are dependent)
P3: ...
P4: ...
C2: ... (from C1, P3, and P4 independently)
```

If some supports for the same conclusion are dependent while others are independent, the parenthetical should say so directly:

```text
C2: ... (from C1 and P3, which are dependent; P4 independently supports C2)
```

This is easy to read, accessible, and compatible with PDF/HTML. Visual diagrams can be included as optional summaries, but they should never contain structural information that is missing from the prose reconstruction.

Need decision: should the book use `+` for dependent/linked support and separate arrows for independent support, or should it use bracket/line notation?

## Relationship To Chapter 3

Chapter 2 should not fully evaluate arguments yet. It can preview evaluation, but the focus is representation. Chapter 3 can then take reconstructed arguments and introduce validity, invalidity, soundness, strength, cogency, and counterexamples more explicitly.

This sequencing fits the exam structure:

1. Identify arguments and conclusions.
2. Reconstruct and diagram.
3. Classify deductive/inductive and evaluate form.
4. Identify fallacies.

## Initial Recommendation

Make Chapter 2 primarily about reconstruction and diagramming. Save most validity/soundness work for Chapter 3, even though Chapter 1 has already previewed the terms. This gives students a slower ramp:

- Chapter 1: What is an argument?
- Chapter 2: What is the structure of this argument?
- Chapter 3: Is the argument any good?

That sequence matches the assignments better than jumping immediately from Chapter 1 into validity and soundness.
