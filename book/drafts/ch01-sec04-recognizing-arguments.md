## 1.4 Recognizing Arguments in the Wild

In the previous three sections we assembled the apparatus. Language does many jobs (§1.1); only some sentences are statements (§1.2); arguments are statements arranged into premises and a conclusion connected by an inferential relation (§1.3). We have what we need.

The trouble is that our use of language doesn't come pre-labeled. Sentences flow into each other without indicator words, or with misleading ones. Two passages that look identical on the page can be doing entirely different jobs. Multiple arguments can be tangled together in one paragraph, with intermediate claims doing double duty as conclusions of one inference and premises of the next. Recognizing arguments in the wild — picking them out of real prose, distinguishing them from things that aren't quite arguments, laying them out so we can examine them — is the skill that turns the apparatus from something you can describe into something you can actually use. And that's our aim. The development of the skills needed to pick out and analyze arguments and the corresponding skills needed to produce good arguments. The answer to the question, "Is it any good?" is not idle. The answer is an active engagement with other people's language and your own. 

### Argument or explanation?

Way back in §1.1 we put *explaining* in the catalog of things people do with language and noted that explanations look a lot like arguments. They use reasons. They have inferential structure. They often use the same indicator words — especially *because*. They can sound, on a first reading, exactly the same. The difference is not in the words. It's in the job the words are doing.

An argument moves you from claims you accept (the premises) to a claim that's in question (the conclusion). The conclusion is the thing the arguer is trying to get you to believe — the part you might not have accepted before. The premises are the reasons offered.

An explanation works in the other direction. The thing being explained is something everyone (in the intended audience) already accepts — it's not in question. The explanation provides the reasons *why* that already-accepted thing is the case. You're not being asked to believe the explanandum; you already do. You're being told why it's true.

(Two pieces of vocabulary, since you'll occasionally see them: the *explanandum* is the thing being explained; the *explanans* is the reason offered. *Premise* and *conclusion* are the parallel words for arguments.)

The same content can serve as either an argument or an explanation, depending on what's in question. Two examples:

> **Argument.** *We should expand the electrical grid, because data centers are being built faster than we can power them.*
>
> **Explanation.** *Electricity prices are rising, because data centers are being built faster than we can power them.*

Same claim after "because". In the first, the conclusion is *we should expand the grid* — a claim that's contested, that depends on policy and money, and that the speaker is trying to bring you around to. The premise is the reason offered: *data centers are being built faster than we can power them.* It's an argument because there's something at issue. 

In the second, the thing to be explained, (the explanandum) is *electricity prices are rising*. That's not in question. Anyone's electric bill has been telling them this for a while. The work being done is to explain *why*. The data-center load isn't being offered as a reason to *believe* prices are rising; it's being offered as a reason *they are*. It's an explanation.

Another pair, even clearer:

> **Argument.** *Your car doesn't have any gas, so it won't start.*
>
> **Explanation.** *My car wouldn't start. Oh, look — I'm out of gas. That's why.*

The first is prospective. It's not yet known that you won't be able to start your car. The speaker is reasoning forward from a known fact (no gas) to a predicted outcome (won't start). It's an argument.

The second is retrospective. The car has already failed to start; that's the data point everyone has. The reason offered (out of gas) is offered to explain the already-observed fact. It's an explanation.

That last contrast points to a useful diagnostic: **arguments are often future- or contention-oriented; explanations are usually past- or already-accepted-oriented.** It isn't a perfect rule. Some arguments are about the past — a historian arguing for one reconstruction of an event over another, for instance, is making an argument about something that already happened, but the conclusion is still in question because the *historical claim* hasn't been settled. And a few explanations gesture forward — *"the storm will be bad because the air pressure is dropping fast"* could be either, depending on whether the listener already accepts that the storm will be bad. But for a quick first pass, time-direction is a strong tell: when in doubt, ask whether the would-be conclusion is something the audience already believes (likely an explanation) or something the speaker is trying to convince them of (likely an argument).

A second diagnostic worth keeping: **argue or make sense of?** If the speaker's posture is *let me convince you of this*, it's an argument. If it's *let me help you make sense of this*, it's an explanation. Same words on the page can do either job, depending on what kind of conversation is happening.

### A procedure for extracting arguments

Here is a workable procedure for pulling an argument out of real text. It looks pedantic written down, but it gets natural, nuanced, and requires judgment as you practice.

1. **Read the passage twice.** Once for the gist; once ready to take notes. 
2. **Find the conclusion.** Ask yourself: what is the writer asking the reader to accept? It's often — not always — the most contentious-sounding sentence in the passage. Look for conclusion indicators (*therefore, thus, so, hence, consequently*) but don't rely on them; conclusions can sit at the start of a passage, at the end, or in the middle.
3. **Find the premises.** Ask: what reasons are given for the conclusion? Look for premise indicators (*because, since, given that, for*) and for sentences whose role is clearly to support something else.
4. **Lay it out in standard form** (P1, P2, … <m>\therefore</m> C). One statement per line. Use exactly the wording the passage uses where possible; reword only when the passage's prose makes the structure hard to see.
5. **Check the structure.** Does the conclusion really follow from the premises? Is something obviously missing — a hidden assumption the passage takes for granted? Are there actually two arguments in this passage instead of one? Does what looks like a single premise turn out to itself be a small argument?

A worked example. Here's a short paragraph:

> *Most people think of running shoes as a recent invention, but archaeologists have found rope-soled sandals from twenty thousand years ago that show specific wear patterns associated with running, and similar sandals have appeared independently in widely separated cultures. So the deliberate design of footwear for running is a much older human practice than people assume.*

Read once for gist: claim about running-shoe history. Read again taking notes/marking up the passage: the conclusion is signaled by *so* — *the deliberate design of footwear for running is a much older human practice than people assume*. The premises are the two historical claims that are assumed to be true: the claims about archaeological discoveries and cultural distribution.

In standard form:

> P1: Archaeologists have found rope-soled sandals from twenty thousand years ago with wear patterns associated with running.
>
> P2: Similar running-adapted sandals have appeared independently in widely separated cultures.
>
> <m>\therefore</m> C: The deliberate design of footwear for running is a much older human practice than people assume. (from P1 and P2)

Two premises, one conclusion, indicator word *so*. The first sentence (*"Most people think…"*) isn't a premise of the argument; it's setting up what the argument is pushing back against. Notice that — sometimes the prose includes orientation that isn't part of the inference, and you should leave it out of the standard-form layout. Though, sometimes, this set-up is so integral to the argument, that it will make it into the conclusion itself. 

### Layered arguments and sub-conclusions

In §1.3 we noted that real arguments are often messier than P1, P2, <m>\therefore</m> C. Sometimes a passage argues for an intermediate claim and then uses that intermediate claim as a premise for a further conclusion. We called these intermediate claims *sub-conclusions*. The notation: each sub-conclusion is labeled C1, C2, … and followed by a parenthetical note saying which premises (and which earlier conclusions) it follows from.

A worked example. Here's a paragraph adapted from a charity pitch:

> *You should donate to Oxfam. Oxfam efficiently uses contributions to help end long-term poverty by providing the tools for people to get an education and support their families. Eighty-nine cents of every dollar goes directly to organizations on the ground. The aid provides for things like goats and schoolbooks, so that a family can provide for itself and the next generation has options. Also, it feels good to help people.*

The conclusion is the first sentence: *you should donate to Oxfam*. Notice the conclusion at the start, not the end — perfectly common. The rest of the paragraph is offering reasons.

But the structure is layered. Some of the reasons are themselves supported by other reasons. Two factual claims (89 cents on the dollar; the aid provides for goats and schoolbooks) support an intermediate claim (Oxfam uses donations efficiently to end long-term poverty). That intermediate claim, plus a separate consideration (it feels good to help people), supports the main conclusion (you should donate). Laid out:

> P1: 89 cents of every dollar goes directly to organizations on the ground.
>
> P2: The aid provides for things like goats and schoolbooks.
>
> C1: The aid Oxfam provides makes it so a family can provide for itself and the next generation has options. *(from P2)*
>
> C2: Oxfam efficiently uses contributions to help end long-term poverty by providing the tools for people to get an education and support their families. *(from P1 and C1)*
>
> P3: It feels good to help people.
>
> <m>\therefore</m> C3: You should donate to Oxfam. *(from C2 and P3)*

This is the diagramming notation we'll use throughout the book whenever an argument has more than one inference in it. Two things worth pausing on.

First, every conclusion declares what it follows from. *(from P2)*, *(from P1 and C1)*, *(from C2 and P3)* — those parenthetical notes are how the structure shows itself. Without them, a multi-step argument is just a list of statements, and the reader has to guess which inferences the writer intended.

Second, this is harder than it looks the first time. The Oxfam paragraph contains six statements doing different jobs — two raw premises, three inferred claims (one of which is the final conclusion), and one independent premise (P3) that doesn't connect to the others until the very end. Pulling that structure out cleanly takes practice. The Field Exercise below has a couple of layered passages so you can practice on something a little messier than the one we just did together.

### Where we're headed

That's the apparatus and the extraction skill. To recap what we've put together over four sections: the recognition that language does many jobs and only some of them count as arguing (§1.1); the precise notion of a statement as a sentence that is either true or false, regardless of who knows or who agrees (§1.2); the structural roles of premise and conclusion, the distinction between intent and successful support, indicator words, and standard form (§1.3); and now (§1.4) the practice of pulling all of that out of real text, distinguishing argument from explanation, and handling layered structure.

What we *can't* yet do is evaluate the arguments we've extracted. We can lay one out cleanly and we can ask the question we've been pointing at since the beginning — *is this any good?* — but we don't yet have the tools to answer that question with rigor.

Two things stand between us and that answer. The first is that even a careful extraction is still rough. Real passages contain background, repeated claims, sentences doing rhetorical rather than logical work, and unstated assumptions the arguer takes for granted. Before we can evaluate an argument, we need to clean it up: represent it in a form fair to the arguer and clear enough to examine. That work is called *reconstruction*, and it's what the next chapter is for. Reconstruction takes the rough extractions we did in this section and turns them into representations careful enough to support real evaluation.

The second thing is the technical machinery for evaluation itself. *Validity* and *soundness* — names you've already seen, definitions still to come — are the precise questions we'll ask of a reconstructed argument: does the conclusion really follow from the premises, and are the premises actually true. The chapters after the next one build that machinery in different domains: categorical logic in Part II, sentential logic and natural deduction in Part III, statistical reasoning in Part IV.

All of that runs on the structures we've built here. Statements as truth-bearing sentences; arguments as premises supporting conclusions; the difference between explanation and argument; the diagrammatic structure of layered reasoning. That's the foundation, and the next chapter is where we'll first lean on it.
