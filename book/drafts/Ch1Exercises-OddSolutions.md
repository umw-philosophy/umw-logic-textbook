## Exercise Set 1: What We Do With Language

**1.** *Asking.* The speaker is requesting that someone send a file. Although phrased as a question ("Could you..."), its dominant function is a polite request for action, not a claim about how things are.

**3.** *Promising.* The speaker is committing to a future action (bringing the handouts tomorrow). This is not asserting that something is the case; it is undertaking an obligation.

**13.** *Arguing.* The conclusion is "you should take the bus to campus." The premise is "parking costs twelve dollars a day." The indicator word *since* flags the premise.

**15.** *Expressing / Greeting.* This is a social expression of goodwill. It does not assert that something is the case and does not offer reasons for a claim.

## Exercise Set 5: Fact, Opinion, and Statementhood

**1.** The classification is wrong. "Factory farming is wrong" asserts that something is the case — that factory farming is wrong. By the chapter's definition, it is a statement with a truth value. Calling it "an opinion" describes whose belief it is, not what kind of sentence it is. A sentence can be both someone's opinion (a belief they hold) and a statement (a sentence that is true or false). The categories collapse into each other.

**3.** This confuses consensus with truth. People disagree about many things that are true or false. Disagreement tells you that the question is unresolved *among people*, not that the sentence lacks a truth value. The sentence asserts that something is the case; it is either true or false regardless of whether anyone has settled the debate.

**5.** This confuses *provability* with *statementhood*. Both sentences assert that something is the case. Both are either true or false. The fact that one is easy to verify and the other is not does not change what kind of sentence each is. The chapter's criterion is whether the sentence says that something is the case, not whether we can prove it.

**7.** This is wrong on two counts. First, "we should raise the minimum wage" is a statement — it asserts that something is the case, and it is either true or false. Second, even if one insists on calling it "an opinion," opinions can serve as premises. Arguments are built out of statements, and the chapter shows that contested claims are still statements. The fact-vs-opinion taxonomy does not determine what can function as a premise.

**9.** This assumes that "matters of taste" are not truth-apt. But "pineapple belongs on pizza" asserts that something is the case. By the chapter's definition and the working assumption of classical logic, assertoric sentences are either true or false. The sentence says that something is the case; it is a statement.

## Exercise Set 9: Modus Ponens and Modus Tollens

**1.** Standard form:
> P1: If the alarm is armed, then opening the door will trigger it.
> P2: The alarm is armed.
> ∴ C: Opening the door will trigger it.

Form: **Modus ponens.** The second premise affirms the antecedent ("the alarm is armed"), and the conclusion affirms the consequent ("opening the door will trigger it"). Antecedent: The alarm is armed. Consequent: Opening the door will trigger it.

**3.** Standard form:
> P1: If the sculpture were made of bronze, then it would not be attracted to a magnet.
> P2: The sculpture is attracted to a magnet.
> ∴ C: The sculpture is not made of bronze.

Form: **Modus tollens.** The second premise denies the consequent ("would not be attracted" is denied by "is attracted"), and the conclusion denies the antecedent. Antecedent: The sculpture were made of bronze. Consequent: It would not be attracted to a magnet.

**5.** Standard form:
> P1: If the course required a textbook, it would appear on the bookstore list.
> P2: The course does not require a textbook.
> ∴ C: It does not appear on the bookstore list.

Form: **Neither.** This argument denies the antecedent, which is a formal fallacy. Modus tollens denies the *consequent*, while modus ponens affirms the *antecedent*. (The course might appear on the bookstore list for some other reason.) Antecedent: The course required a textbook. Consequent: It would appear on the bookstore list.

**7.** Standard form:
> P1: If class is canceled, then the assignment deadline will be extended.
> P2: Class is canceled.
> ∴ C: The assignment deadline will be extended.

Form: **Modus ponens.** The second premise affirms the antecedent. Antecedent: Class is canceled. Consequent: The assignment deadline will be extended.

**9.** Standard form:
> P1: If the study abroad program requires a 3.0 GPA, then students below 3.0 are ineligible.
> P2: The program requires a 3.0 GPA.
> ∴ C: Students below 3.0 are ineligible.

Form: **Modus ponens.** The second premise affirms the antecedent. Antecedent: The study abroad program requires a 3.0 GPA. Consequent: Students below 3.0 are ineligible.

## Exercise Set 11: Extracting Arguments From Realistic Prose

**1.** Conclusion: Students should visit office hours early in the semester.

Standard form:
> P1: Early visits make it easier to ask for help later.
> P2: Early visits give professors a better sense of what students are finding confusing.
> ∴ C: Students should visit office hours early in the semester.

Note: The first sentence ("Most students think office hours are only for emergencies, but that is a mistake") is orientation — it sets up what the argument pushes back against but is not itself a premise of the inference.

**3.** Conclusion: We should replace the old projector in Monroe Hall.

Standard form:
> P1: The projector cuts out during class.
> P2: Replacement bulbs are no longer easy to order.
> P3: Several instructors have stopped using media clips because they cannot trust the equipment.

**5.** Conclusion: The committee should not postpone the vote again.

**7.** Conclusion: College athletes should be allowed to profit from their name, image, and likeness.

## Exercise Set 12: Layered Arguments and Sub-Conclusions

**1.** Standard form:
> P1: Many students who need tutoring work during the day.
> P2: Students who work during the day cannot attend most current tutoring sessions.
> C1: The current schedule excludes many of the students who need help. *(from P1 and P2)*
> P3: Expanding evening tutoring would make academic support available to more students.
> ∴ C2: The university should expand evening tutoring. *(from C1 and P3)*

**3.** Standard form:
> P1: Drivers often fail to see pedestrians at the crosswalk at night.
> P2: Poor visibility makes collisions more likely.
> C1: The crosswalk is dangerous at night. *(from P1 and P2)*
> P3: Better lighting would improve visibility.
> P4: Improving visibility would make the crosswalk safer.
> ∴ C2: The city should install better lighting near the crosswalk. *(from C1, P3, and P4)*

**5.** Standard form:
> P1: The software collects sensitive data.
> P2: Sensitive data can be misused or exposed in a breach.
> C1: The software creates serious privacy risks. *(from P1 and P2)*
> P3: Tools that create serious privacy risks should be avoided unless they are necessary.
> P4: Professors can take attendance without location tracking.
> C2: This software is not necessary. *(from P4)*
> ∴ C3: We should not use attendance software that tracks students' locations. *(from C1, P3, and C2)*

**7.** Standard form:
> P1: Students arrive with widely varying reasoning skills.
> P2: A shared course would give everyone a common vocabulary for evaluating arguments.
> C1: A common vocabulary makes class discussions in other courses more productive. *(from P2)*
> P3: More productive discussions improve learning across the curriculum.
> ∴ C2: We should require all first-year students to take a critical thinking course. *(from P1, C1, and P3)*

> ∴ C: Students must list those notes in their acknowledgments.

**7.** *Explanation plus a statement.* The first sentence is an explanation: the explanandum is "the classroom was moved" (already known); the explanans is "the projector broke." **9.** *Argument.* Contains a conditional: antecedent = "the committee meets on Friday"; consequent = "the proposal will be voted on before the deadline." The argument denies the consequent (modus tollens). Indicator: *Therefore*.

Standard form:
